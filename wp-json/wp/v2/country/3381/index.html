Per the national curriculum, students must master fast, joined handwriting by the end of Year 6. The most common style taught in England is the modern cursive, which can be semi-connected or fully joined.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Education in England is overseen by the United Kingdom\u2019s Department for Education (DfE). Local government authorities are responsible for implementing the department\u2019s policies at state-funded schools, which serve 93% of all students, while private schools enjoy some flexibility in curriculum and structure. Schooling up to 16 years is compulsory in England, and state-funded education system is divided into Key Stages \u2014 Early Years Foundation Stage (3\u20134 years old), Primary comprising Key Stages 1 (5\u20136 years old) and Stage 2 (7\u201310 years old), Secondary comprising Key Stages 3 (11\u201313 years old) and 4 (14\u201315 years old), and finally Key Stage 5 (16\u201317 years old).<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The National Curriculum in England (NCE), published by the DfE, sets out what must be taught in all subjects in local-authority-maintained schools. The latest version of the curriculum was published in 2014. While it doesn\u2019t specify particular models for handwriting teaching, the NCE stipulates that students start with uppercase unjoined styles in preschool, and by the end of Year 6 (10\u201311 years old), master fast, joined handwriting.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
