The national curriculum dictates that students must learn to write legibly by hand between Years 1 and 3. While no model is prescribed, nowadays there is marked preference towards textad stil<\/span>, or print script.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Education in Sweden is overseen by three main government authorities: the Utbildnings\u00addepartementet<\/em>, or Ministry of Education and Research, is responsible for education and research policies; the Skolinspektionen<\/em>, or the Swedish School Inspectorate, oversees school quality and monitors compliance to the Education Act; and the Skolverket<\/em>, or National Agency of Education, provides information about education, and administers public funding and grants.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Children start with f\u00f6rskoleklass<\/em>, or preschool, also known as Year 0. This is followed by l\u00e5gstadiet<\/em>, or primary school comprising Years 1\u20133; mellanstadiet<\/em>, or middle school that is Years 4\u20136; and h\u00f6gstadiet<\/em>, or high school, consisting of Years 7\u20139. All these stages of education are compulsory. Compulsory education also includes sameskolor<\/em>, or Sami schools, for indigenous Sami children. Gymnasium,<\/em> or upper secondary school, i.e. Grades 10-11, is optional.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

School education is tuition-free, and tax-financed. Since 1992, parents can choose to send their children to either municipal schools, or to friskola<\/em>, or independent schools, that also depend on public funding.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
