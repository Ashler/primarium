The Dutch-French linguistic and cultural divide in Belgium is mirrored in traditions of handwriting education. Regional preferences in terms of handwriting models show clear links to the Netherlands and France.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Belgium is divided into three communities on linguistic lines: the Vlaamse Gemeenschap<\/em>, or Flemish Community; Communaut\u00e9 fran\u00e7aise<\/em>, or French Community; and Deutschsprachige Gemeinschaft<\/em>, or German-speaking Community. Each of these communities governs different geographical regions based on language use. Since 1989, the administration of education is also accordingly divided. La F\u00e9d\u00e9ration Wallonie-Bruxelles<\/em>, or Wallonia-Brussels Federation, is responsible for education in the French-speaking areas of the Walloon Region. In the Vlaams Gewest<\/em>, or Flemish Region (also known as Flanders), education falls under the purview of the Vlaams Ministerie van Onderwijs en Vorming<\/em>, or Flemish Ministry of Education and Training. Both institutions share jurisdiction over the Brussels Capital Region, while education in the small German-speaking province of Li\u00e8ge in Walloon is looked after by Das Ministerium der Deutschsprachigen Gemeinschaft<\/em>, or the Ministry of the German-speaking Community.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
