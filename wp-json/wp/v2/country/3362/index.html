Between Grades 1\u20133, students learn upright and slanted cursive letters, as part of the m\u1eabu ch\u1eef th\u1ea3o ti\u1ebfng vi\u1ec7t<\/span>, or Vietnamese official cursive script, which was adopted in 2002. No print script letters are taught.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In Vietnam, the B\u1ed9 Gi\u00e1o d\u1ee5c v\u00e0 \u0110\u00e0o t\u1ea1o<\/em>, or Ministry of Education and Training, is responsible for education at all levels. It sets out broad policy guidelines, while its Centre for Curriculum Development and General Methodology of Education handles all curriculum research and development. Vietnamese is the national language, and written in the Latin script. The Vietnamese alphabet is known as Qu\u1ed1c ng\u1eef<\/em> locally, and accents are used to represent tones in the language. Since 2008, English is also a compulsory subject from Grade 3.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Children start with preschool (3 months\u20136 years old) followed by primary education comprising Grade 1\u20135 (6\u201310 years old), and then lower secondary, comprising Grade 6\u20139 (11\u201314 years old), and finally upper secondary, comprising Grade 10\u201312 (15\u201317 years old). Of these, primary education is free and compulsory, and students are obligated to attend school till the end of lower secondary. Schools are primarily state-funded and public, though private schools also exist.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The Ministry publishes the General Education Curriculum, which must be followed by all schools, whether public or private. The last iteration was released in 2018, with the goal of being implemented in primary schools by 2020-21.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
