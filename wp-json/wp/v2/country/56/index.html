Students usually learn print script in first grade and cursive writing in second or third grade. In the absence of a unified national directive, approaches to handwriting education vary widely, and are all produced and supported by private <\/span>companies.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The U.S. Department of Education establishes policy for and administers most federal assistance to education, but education is largely a state and local responsibility. The education system is divided into elementary school from Kindergarten to Grade 5 (5\u201311 years old), middle school from Grade 6\u20138 (11\u201314 years old), and high school from Grade 9\u201312 (14\u201318 years old), though it can vary slightly from state to state. Similarly, schooling, though compulsory, can be so for a range between 5\u20138 years to 16\u201318 years old, and the mandatory requirement can be satisfied in public or state-certified private schools, or an approved home school program.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The Common Core State Standards Initiative was introduced in the United States in 2010 to establish standards and goals of teaching in the country. It was sponsored by the National Governors Association (NGA) and Council of Chief State School Officers (CCSSO). The standard does not recommend or regulate specific models for teaching handwriting, and also allows states and schools to determine what models and methods they would like to use for this purpose. States can accept or reject Common Core, as well as add their own state-specific requirements to the specification. Several states have, for instance, added a cursive requirement to their implementation of Common Core.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The general practice nowadays is to teach print letters, usually based on a sans serif typeface (a style known as \u201cmanuscript\u201d in the United States) to first grade students (5\u20136 years old) followed by cursive writing to second or third grade students (7\u20139 years old).<\/p>\n\n\n\n
