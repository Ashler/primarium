Redondilla escolar,<\/em> a vertical cursive writing style predominant in South America at large, has emerged as the most commonly used handwriting teaching model in Per\u00fa.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In Per\u00fa, the Ministerio de Educaci\u00f3n de Per\u00fa<\/em>, or Ministry of Education, formulates, implements and oversees national education policies. After initial education, which comprises nursery for children under 3 years old and kindergarten for those aged 3\u20136 years old, students enter primary education, which goes upto the age of 12, followed by secondary, which goes upto 18. Both primary and secondary education are compulsory. Public education is free for all, and is seen as a public service provided by the state, and while private schools exist in Per\u00fa, they are attended by a very small minority of students.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The Ministerio de Educaci\u00f3n<\/em> last published the Curr\u00edculo Nacional de la Educaci\u00f3n B\u00e1sica<\/em>, or the National Curriculum of Basic Education, in 2016, and Rutas del aprendizaje, material para docentes<\/em>, or Learning routes, material for teachers, three years prior. Of the two, only the latter mentions handwriting, albeit in brief. It specifies that emphasis needs to be on students understanding how the writing system works, rather than on being able to write in cursive. It also espouses the use of uppercase print style letters at the start of literacy education due to their simple, distinct shapes and easy recognisability.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
