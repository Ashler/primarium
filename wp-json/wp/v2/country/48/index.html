The majority of primary schools in Spain maintain the tradition of teaching vertical cursive writing, sometimes with cursive uppercase letters but more frequently with simplified print ones.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

In Spain, education falls under the purview of the Ministerio de Educaci\u00f3n y Formaci\u00f3n Profesional<\/em>, or the Ministry of Education and Vocational Training, that is responsible for policymaking and implementation for all levels of education except university. Its governance is decentralised, and regional authorities oversee primary education in their local area.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

After the Franco regime (1939\u20131975), education was identified as one of the crucial goals for social and political change in Spain, and the system underwent successive reforms to ensure common learning, and to expand access to quality education. This process was completed in 1990 with the approval of the Ley de Ordenaci\u00f3n General del Sistema Educativo,<\/em> or the General Education System Ordering Law, which was most recently revised in 2006, 2013, and 2020.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Colegio,<\/em> or primary education (6\u201312 years old), and Educaci\u00f3n Secundaria Obligatoria<\/em> (ESO), or secondary education (12\u201316 years old), together form the basic phase, which lasts ten years and is compulsory. Before this, children attend Escuela infantil<\/em>, or kindergarten (3\u20136 years old), which may be offered for free by the State in some schools. There are three types of schools in the country: public, private, and those subsidised in part by the government.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
