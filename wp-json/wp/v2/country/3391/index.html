Despite decentralized governance of education, students learn to read and write in a consistent manner across Argentina. They start with simplified printed letters followed by cursive writing.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

The Argentinian education system has four stages. They aren\u2019t exactly the same throughout the country, but in general, the first three \u2014 initial level (45 days\u20135 years old), primary (6\u201312 years old), secondary (13\u201317 years old) \u2014 make up school education, and the fourth, known as superior education, covers postsecondary and university. The Ley de Educaci\u00f3n Nacional 26.006<\/em>, or National Education Law 26.006<\/em>, passed in 2005, regulates the education system and states that education is a public good and a personal and social right that the State must guarantee. It also makes education for children aged 4\u201318 years old mandatory.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The 1993 educational reform and 1994 constitutional reform significantly shaped education and curricula in the country. These reforms unified the education system nationwide in terms of structure, goals, and outcomes. However, the management and funding of schools were left to the provinces. As a result, provinces enjoy the freedom to create their own teaching resources for classrooms. This has created inequalities between schools across the country due to the wealth disparity of different regions.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
