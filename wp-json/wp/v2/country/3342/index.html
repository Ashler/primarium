The most common handwriting model in classrooms is verbonden schrift<\/em>, a slanted continuous cursive with loops, though blokschrift<\/em>, an unjoined simplified print script, has gained popularity. Teachers can choose either or both, based on their discretion.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n\n

Dutch education is governed by a series of bodies that operate under the Ministerie van Onderwijs, Cultuur en Wetenschappen<\/em> (OCW), or Ministry Education, Culture and Science. These are Dienst Uitvoering Onderwijs<\/em> (DUO), or Education Executive Agency, the Directie Primair Onderwijs<\/em>, or Primary Education Directorate, and the Stichting Leerplan Ontwikkeling<\/em> (SLO), or National Expertise Center for the Curriculum.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

There are both public and private schools in the Netherlands. Public schools are run by the government, while private schools are independently managed and generally based on religious or ideological principles. Education starts with optional daycare, followed by primary school, then secondary school that can be vocational or focused on preparatory teaching for university, and finally higher education. Primary school has eight grades, locally known as groepen<\/em>. Education is compulsory from Groep 2<\/em> until the age of sixteen.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The SLO publishes a series of texts and goals that define knowledge, skills and attitude objectives for each learning area. The last iteration was published in 2021. Schools use them as a framework on whose foundation they develop their own specific curricula and teaching methods. For the learning area of writing, the instructions go no further than students needing to achieve \u201clegible writing\u201d. No details are provided about the handwriting models that should be used for teaching, or the grades when this instruction should take place. In the absence of guidelines from the OCW, teachers depend on handwriting instruction systems developed by private publishers such as Zwijsen, Noordhoff Uitgevers and Malmberg.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
