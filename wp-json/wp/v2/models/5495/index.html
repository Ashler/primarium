Marion Richardson (1892\u20131946), a former student of Edward Johnston (1872\u20131944) and exponent of the italic revival, developed a set of cards for handwriting instruction called Dudley Writing Cards<\/em> in the 1930s with advice from Johnston and Alfred Fairbank (1895\u20131982). She showed cursive letters with small connecting strokes that drew from Johnston\u2019s exploration of the skeletal shapes of letterforms.<\/p>\n\n\n

In 1935, she published Writing and Writing Patterns<\/em> in 1935. This book builds on her observations about the importance of fine motor development in teaching handwriting. In it, handwriting instruction was accompanied by motor exercises based on patterns resulting from the repetition of six elementary forms related to tracing the alphabet.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Marion Richardson\u2019s approach was quite successful, and at the end of the 20th century, it was one of the most widespread models used in England schools. In her book, Handwriting of the twentieth century<\/em>, Rosemary Sassoon describes Richardson\u2019s contributions to writing development one of the most significant.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
